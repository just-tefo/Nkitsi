The feedback provided in this section is based on your responses to ADEPT-15, the work-related behavioral questionnaire. Along with our values and the situations we find ourselves in, our behavior influences how others see us and how we tend to respond at work.

Learnability
Learnability describes your approach to seeking continual self-improvement.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that you may be open to asking for constructive feedback to help understand your strengths and limitations. At times, you may prefer to leverage your strengths rather than gain new skills. This supports your continuous learning, however, you may sometimes miss opportunities to reflect on job experiences.

 Build on this further:

  • Push beyond your comfort zone by seeking "stretch" assignments that require you to learn new skills and interact with new people. Meet with your supervisor or peers to discuss what you did well and areas for improvement. Using this feedback, generate strategies for development.

  • Learn from experiences. When dealing with a new issue or a particularly challenging problem, ask yourself, "What is the lesson to be learned here? Have there been early clues that I may have missed or ignored?" Reflect on these questions and identify how you could handle the situation differently.
Agility
Agility describes your approach to adapting to changing situations.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that you tend to view change positively, and are generally open to shifting between multiple activities and adapting to new situations. There may be times at work, however, that feel more comfortable leveraging established procedures and processes.

 Build on this further:

  • Generate alternative solutions. Practice developing options and alternatives to your proposals in the event that your first suggestion is not acceptable to other decision makers. Evaluate how each of the alternatives will impact the requirements, process, and outcome of the proposal.

  • Identify some individuals who are good at adapting to new situations. Ask them what they do to effectively handle change and successfully perform in an ambiguous environment. Select three techniques they use and identify a project you can apply those to.

Curiosity
Curiosity describes your approach to new initiatives and experiences.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that you are likely to demonstrate an openness and enthusiasm towards new experiences within your areas of expertise. However, when it comes to areas that you are less familiar with at work, you may prefer to solve well-defined, rather than novel problems.

 Build on this further:

  • Constantly expose yourself to new trends and approaches in your area of work. Get into the habit of reading articles on current events, technical journals for new developments, and books for perspectives. Keep a log of viewpoints and trends you think may be of use.

  • When working with others, seek out diverse insights and recommendations. Ask people from different educational backgrounds and job areas to give their viewpoint on a problem or topic, and identify opportunities to incorporate these into your projects.
Determination
Determination describes your approach to setting priorities and achieving goals.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that you may typically exhibit strong drive and persistence to achieve your goals on time, even ones that may be challenging. At work, you are likely to set clear priorities and take appropriate action in uncertain and rapidly changing situations.

 Build on this further:

  • Consider the value of setting goals as a motivational tool. Practice setting small, short-term objectives and milestones to give yourself early success at achieving goals. Over time, try taking on more complex and challenging goals.

  • Focus on organizing the tasks you have to complete to ensure that you are not rushing to meet the deadline. Prioritize the most urgent tasks and set timelines for each task by breaking up big deliverables to manageable pieces.

Work-Related Behaviors
Collaboration
Collaboration describes your approach to working with others and building relationships.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that you may prefer working on tasks independently, and may be less likely to ask for help. As a result, you may seek fewer opportunities to work with others on common goals, which may limit you from building trusting relationships with your colleagues.

 Build on this further:

  • Ask for help. When you are faced with a particularly complex or sensitive issue, reach out to someone with expertise in the area for support rather than trying to tackle it alone. Seek input, suggestions, coaching or guidance even if the ultimate responsibility still resides with you.

  • Establish common ground with peers or colleagues. Look for things that unite you, such as shared experiences and interests and use these to create a sense of partnership. As you work to achieve shared goals and objectives, try expressing your support verbally, as well as through your actions.
Innovation
Innovation describes your approach to generating and implementing new ideas.
Your preferred style:

Your responses indicate that while you may notice potential limitations and problems at work, you prefer to focus more on execution and practical approaches. As a result, you may be less confident in generating or adopting new ideas or implementing process improvements.

 Build on this further:

  • Look out for products, services, or current procedures that could use an improvement. Brainstorm ideas for addressing problems you see and improving the process or product. Share your ideas with your supervisor or peers and ask for their input and suggestions.

  • Consult with others who have been successful in implementing new ideas. Ask them about their general strategies and about how they initiated the process. Try to apply these techniques when implementing your own ideas. Report back to them on how these techniques worked (or did not work) for you.
